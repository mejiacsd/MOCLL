Exploring the urban metabolism of San Diego: Mapping to build resilience and climate adaptation planning using drone technologies

The Department of Geography at San Diego State University (San Diego, California) has partnered with the Universidad Autonoma de Baja California (Tijuana, Mexico) to collect data using drone detection technologies. In order to identify access to water and water quality, burn trash, tires, abandoned furniture, illegal trash sites (abandoned construction material), waste incineration, uninhabited terrain contained with solid waste, earthquake and/or flood crisis planning for risk assessment.

Today, more than ever there is a great variety of technological tools to geo-reference urban elements with which they allow scientists to have a precise location to analyze the environment with better results. In the Department of Geography, at San Diego State University we use two open access software for the creation of data in the field using software such as Footpath, Dioptra, and Open Street Map (influenced by Secondary Cities). Moreover, we would like to collect aerial images with an unmanned aerial drone ship, to process with the technique of photogrammetry to create the mosaics and finally use ArcGIS to make maps with the terrestrial and aerial images. The project will collect material during the fieldwork that will be carried out in San Diego County along the US-Mexico border, with an emphasis on the areas characterized by their concentration of waste and other conditions that surround it. The creation of data consist of two aspects; The first is the land tour in areas with a canal or tributary channels to the San Diego River, as well as the Tijuana River, this will allow us to capture detailed images of the conditions of the visited area, in addition to dimensioning the reality of irregular waste dumps and in the large amounts in which it accumulates within the channels. The second consists of capturing the photometry of the areas to have a broader “panoramic” perspective of the terrain that is explored, so that in the work they will be able to observe a comparison with google images from the same year.

This study will allow us to create data but also to obtain an integral vision of the problems that surround the issue of waste in San Diego County along the US-Mexico border, where the most outstanding are the images that show the informal processes to disintegrate garbage and other waste. As an example, the burning and abandonment of garbage in the canal or surrounding environment, has become part of the city’s landscape, normalizing its presence. Our main objective is to obtain geospatial data, which can be freely accessed by everyone and used by public decision makers and citizens alike. The Tijuana-San Diego cross border area, the Department of Geography at San Diego State University and the Universidad Autonoma de Baja California, are collaborating to offer a remote workshop “Disaster Response and Aerial Technologies” in which problems that are shared by this region are studied, as well as all kinds of disasters that can occur and their possible solutions based on the application of knowledge. Derived from this our project focuses on mapping with the data obtained in three aspects: Access to water and water quality, Illegal trash sites, Earthquake and/or flood crisis planning for risk assessment. Our project will disseminate best practices rapidly, strengthen financing mechanisms for cities, address hotspot areas in need in San Diego County along the US-Mexico border, and ensure social protection (vulnerable areas) with no access to resources and services. For more information please contact Dr. Alma Navarro, beatriznavarro@uabc.edu.mx or Dr. Gabriela Fernandez, at gfernandez2@sdsu.edu.
